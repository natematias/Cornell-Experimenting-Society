Weekly Workload

Before each class, I will assign two readingsâ€“ usually one newspaper article and one academic article that I expect you to read before class. As part of class participation, you will submit a reaction comment on one of the readings to Slack and respond to at least one other student's comment by 9pm Eastern the night before class.

Assignments in the class unfold in three parts which each have a distinct workload. In the first part of the class (Jan-Feb), you will do individual or pair assignment each week. This will sometimes involve writing, sometimes analysis, and sometimes both. In the second part (March), you will form teams of 3-4 students and develop a project proposal. In the final part (April), you will work with your team to complete the final project. For special topics (like bandit algorithms), I may assign you to work in your pairs again.

Weekly assignments are due on Tuesdays at 9pm Eastern unless otherwise noted.

In lectures/discussions, we will discuss a topic within experimentation and the ethics/governance of behavioral research.

In workshops, we will focus more on technical topics, statistical methods, and discussions about your final projects.

I will occasionally offer short quizzes to gauge inform how quickly we go through technical topics in workshop sessions.

Final Project

This class has a two-part final:

  • A group project, due on May 18 by 9pm
  • A final personal essay, due on May 18 by 9pm

The final project will either be an analysis of data from the Upworthy Archive or a new design for a field experiment that you have developed with a community partner (such as The Lab of Ornithology).

In the final personal essay (1200-1500 words), you will pick one of the questions about ethics and governance of behavioral research that we have discussed in class and write about your group project from that perspective. The essay should be your own work and should include a list of references.

The midterm for this class is group project proposal and a proposal for your personal essay.

Participation in Discussions and Workshops

Please post discussions about readings and questions about the course to our chatroom on slack, comm4940.slack.com. Please use your Cornell University email address when requesting to join the workspace.

Whenever the course has assigned reading for a session, you are expected to post at least one top-level comment and one reply to Slack. Participation will be 20% of your course grade.

Here is how we will use channels:

  • #readings: please post your comment and your reply to this channel. This space will be evaluated for your participation grade.
  • #class-sessions: this room is for live discussion/lectures and for discussions related to workshop sessions. Please use threads as much as possible to make it easier for fellow students to navigate
  • #cocalc: this room is for discussion of cocalc issues
  • #class-logistics: is for discussion of class and syllabus issues, as well as general technical challenges
  • #random: is for news links to relevant content you're finding online and general encouragement to your fellow students
  • team-specific rooms are for coordinating with your team and sharing project-specific questions to the professor

Team Progress Reports

During the project period of the class, teams will submit on Canvas a weekly progress report no more than one page long, as group homework. This progress report will be graded. Reports should include the following details (a sample template is available here):

  • What your team made progress on
  • What your team is doing next
  • Who contributed what
  • Where your team is stuck
  • Any updates to your timeline

Group Work and Academic Integrity

I expect students to follow the Cornell University Code of Academic Integrity. You should submit your work as your own, cite sources and outside assistance, and credit people for their contributions.

This class includes group work and individual assignments. On group projects, you are encouraged to work together on the activities for the class, but you are only only able to put your name on projects to which you made an intellectual contribution. If you have any doubts about what is appropriate, ask me.

Grace Period

Late Submissions: All assignments have an automatic one-day grace period. On time and early papers are always encouraged and will be graded the same week. Students can turn in the assignment up to a day late, no questions asked, with the expectation that it could take substantially longer to receive a grade and feedback. After that, an automatic one grade (A to B, B to C, etc) is dropped on the assignment.

It is common for students to experience stressful events at some point during graduate school. Students sometimes experience depression, anxiety, family stress, the loss of loved ones, financial strain, and other stressors. It is perfectly normal for students to seek the service of mental health professionals to provide them with support and skills to cope with these experiences. Values For Resilience, Spring 2020

While plans can be disrupted, values are helpful tools to guide decision-making when things go wrong at unpredictable times. So as we resume classes, I want you to know what my values will be.

First, I will prioritize the class learning goals (see the syllabus index). I am confident that by the end of the semester, each of you will still be able to understand, design, analyze, and critique field experiments and their role in society.

Above all I want to be available to students in a supportive and timely way.

I will prioritize consistency in how we interact, try to minimize sources of unpredictability, and communicate changes well in advance and on the syllabus.

I will prioritize accessibility in class materials, assignments, and how I facilitate discussion. I will organize class as much as possible to support everyone equally, accommodating a range of locations and logistical needs. I will also build  redundancy into class processes.

In assessment, I will continue to prioritize participation, mutual support, and growth. My assessment goal is to provide the feedback you need to grow; not to add stress by measuring you up on an imaginary pre-pandemic scale. Since each studentâ€™s learning depends on discussion and collaboration with others, I will continue to assess asynchronous participation.

The weekly schedule has been adjusted to include discussion time for students in Asia and Europe. See the syllabus for details.

Can We Still Do Our Project?

The new deadline for project proposals is Tuesday, April 14. Projects involving designing field experiments are likely impossible now. I strongly encourage teams to shift (if you haven't already) to the default Upworthy Archive project. but if you think you can finish your original project idea, contact me ASAP.

What Happens To Our Team?

Since there is minimal time remaining in the semester, teams should stick with the group they have already chosen. I may permit a small number of swaps for time zone issues if you contact me by April 8th. Any later, and it would be unfair to team members.

How Can We Collaborate Remotely?

Coordination: I have created an online chat room for each team and encourage you to coordinate there.

Documents & Files: Cornell has advised that firewall issues may prevent students from accessing Google Docs or in some cases even email. To aid in collaboration, I have created CoCalc projects for each team. I strongly encourage you to use your CoCalc project for team collaboration since it is widely accessible and I can access when grading. I have created a repository of template documents for team collaboration on CoCalc and have added it to each project.

Documents in CoCalc are written using Markdown, which creates formatted documents from a few simple text codes. You can learn more about Markdown here.

We Prefer To Use Google Docs/Drive

If you want to use Google Docs/Drive, you must first confirm that every team member will have consistent access to the service throughout the semester. If I have confirmation in chat from every team member, I will create a folder for the team. That will prevent situations where the professor doesn't have read access to documents he is supposed to grade.


How Will We Get Updates?

I will post all class-wide updates to students via Canvas, alongside a thread in Slack.


Contingency Plans

  • If you have technical problems, please communicate with the professor via slack or email ASAP so he can make accommodations
  • If the professor is incapacitated, the class will be continued by Professor Neil Lewis Jr. and Max Klein.
  • If you are incapacitated and unable to continue the class, please:
    • Contact the professor, who will likely encourage you to take an incomplete
    • If you have time, communicate to your team members
